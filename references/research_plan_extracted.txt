MLLM-Driven Automated Layer Labeling,
Metrology, and Anomaly Detection in
Semiconductor and Display Array
Electron Micrographs
Introduction to the Shifting Paradigm in
Microelectronic Metrology
The relentless scaling of microelectronic architectures, ranging from advanced semiconductor
logic nodes to complex Low-Temperature Polycrystalline Silicon (LTPS) display arrays, has
driven structural geometries into extreme sub-micron and nanometer regimes. At these scales,
the characterization of multi-layered thin-film stacks relies entirely on high-resolution imaging
modalities, primarily scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy
(TEM).1 These electron micrographs capture the physical reality of the fabricated device,
revealing the precise morphological details of dielectric insulators, metallic interconnects,
semiconductor channels, and organic planarization layers.2 However, the extraction of
actionable metrological data from these images has historically remained a profoundly manual,
labor-intensive, and subjective process.1
In a conventional failure analysis (FA) or inline metrology workflow, a human engineer must
meticulously scrutinize a micrograph, correlate the visual gray-level striations with a known
schematic or design table, manually draw boundaries to annotate distinct layers, and utilize
point-to-point software tools to estimate layer thicknesses.1 Furthermore, the engineer must rely
on domain expertise to identify morphological anomalies, voids, or delamination events.1 This
manual pipeline introduces substantial latency into the manufacturing feedback loop, with
end-to-end failure analysis—from failure verification through physical analysis to root-cause
reporting—traditionally consuming days to weeks of expert time.1
The emergence of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), which possess architectures
capable of natively processing both complex textual inputs and high-resolution visual data,
presents a transformative solution to this metrological bottleneck.6 By leveraging models such
as GPT-4 Turbo with Vision, LLaMA-3.2-11B-Vision-Instruct, and specialized domain-adapted
frameworks like FabGPT and MicroscopyGPT, the industry is pivoting toward fully autonomous,
agentic analytical workflows.6 The overarching objective is to deploy these MLLMs to ingest a
user-provided prompt—specifically, an image featuring a designated region of interest (ROI)
enclosed in a red rectangle, accompanied by supporting textual or tabular data—and
autonomously execute semantic layer labeling that mimics or exceeds human deductive
reasoning.6
Beyond mere semantic labeling, the integration of these MLLMs with deterministic physical
measurement algorithms, open-vocabulary segmentation foundation models, and multi-agent
orchestration frameworks facilitates the autonomous estimation of layer thickness and the
execution of rigorous anomaly detection.1 This comprehensive report delivers an exhaustive
analysis of the cognitive, algorithmic, and computational mechanisms required to deploy MLLMs
for automated electron micrograph analysis. By examining a specific LTPS array cross-section
as a foundational case study, this analysis dissects the visual reasoning capabilities of MLLMs,
the transition from traditional convolutional neural networks to advanced segmentation
architectures, the mathematical basis of intensity profile metrology, and the deployment of
agentic pipelines for prognostic failure analysis.
Contextual Reasoning and Multimodal Data Fusion
The fundamental advantage of an MLLM over traditional machine vision systems lies in its
capacity for generalized cognitive reasoning and its ability to synthesize disparate data
modalities.8 Traditional machine vision, relying on static pixel classifiers, fails when confronted
with the ambiguity of electron micrographs, where different materials may yield identical
gray-level contrast.11 To execute automated layer labeling within a targeted ROI, the MLLM must
replicate the holistic deductive process of a human engineer, which involves cross-referencing
the visual image with structured supporting information.

Resolving the Ambiguity of Indistinguishable Boundaries

The critical metrological challenge explicitly highlighted in this exemplar is that adjacent
inorganic layers (e.g., Buffer, GI1, GI2, ILD, PV) and adjacent organic layers (e.g., PLN1, PLN2,
PLN3, Bank1, Bank2) exhibit virtually identical gray-level contrast within the SEM micrograph.
Consequently, their physical boundaries cannot be visually distinguished based purely on pixel
intensity.
To autonomously label the red rectangle, the MLLM must replicate the human methodology
outlined in the supporting text: it must identify the layers by cross-referencing their composition,
relative color (contrast), and relative thickness against the provided design table. The MLLM
begins by anchoring its analysis on the high-contrast elements. The heavy metals, such as the
Molybdenum gate electrodes (GE1, GE2) and the Ti/Al/Ti source/drain interconnects (SD1,
SD2), scatter electrons heavily and appear as distinct, bright white bands within the
cross-section.8 The model identifies the lowest bright band as GE1, the intermediate bright
bands as GE2 and SD1, and the upper massive bright structure as SD2.
Once these high-contrast anchors are established, the MLLM utilizes spatial reasoning and
sequence priors to deduce the location of the indistinguishable dark layers.6 The model reads
the table to understand that the dark region immediately beneath the GE1 anchor must be the
GI1 layer, and the ultra-thin band below that must be the Poly-Si. Furthermore, when confronted
with the massive dark expanse above SD2, the MLLM refers to the table to deduce that this
region contains PLN2/3, the Anode, and Bank1/Bank2. By calculating the ratio of the design
thicknesses (e.g., recognizing that PLN2/3 is 2700 nm while Bank1 is only 500 nm), the MLLM
can estimate the proportionate spatial location of these conceptually distinct layers even in the
absence of a visible boundary, mapping the textual labels to the corresponding coordinate
space within the red rectangle.6 This demonstrates a profound level of cognitive fusion, where
the model compensates for visual deficiencies by imposing logical constraints derived from
tabular text.
The Agentic Pipeline for Automated Labeling
Translating this cognitive reasoning into an automated software pipeline requires a sophisticated
agentic framework. In highly scaled manufacturing environments, the time taken to process
visual information is critical.1 The workflow for labeling the layers within the designated red
rectangle leverages the concepts of in-context learning, multimodal prompting, and speculative
execution.
Multimodal Instruction Tuning and Prompt Architecture

To ensure the MLLM correctly interprets the semiconductor micrograph, it must be conditioned
through physics-aware instruction tuning.16 Frameworks such as QuPAINT and datasets like
QMat-Instruct have been developed to provide MLLMs with multimodal, physics-aware
question-answer pairs.16 This training teaches the model to understand the distinct visual
signatures produced by specific material compositions under an electron beam.13 During
inference, the model is tested on its ability to recognize that high-Z (high atomic number)
elements appear bright in Z-contrast imaging, while low-Z elements like organic polymers or

         appear dark.8
The human operator initiates the process by supplying the image, drawing the red rectangle
bounding box, and providing the supporting data table as a combined prompt.9 The prompt
architecture utilizes few-shot or one-shot learning techniques.9 By providing a previous example
where a human has manually drawn lines connecting textual labels to specific striations, the
model understands the structural format required for the output.6 The red rectangle acts as a
powerful spatial prior, acting as an attention mechanism that forces the model's transformer
layers to calculate pixel similarities and semantic features exclusively within that bounded
region, ignoring extraneous background data.9
Overcoming Agentic Depth and Sequential Latency

Querying an MLLM to perform multi-step reasoning—first parsing the table, then locating the
metal anchors, then deducing the organic sequences, and finally projecting textual labels onto
the image coordinates—introduces substantial computational overhead. This cascaded loop of
perception, reasoning, and tool invocation is referred to as "agentic depth".15 In a
high-throughput failure analysis laboratory, prohibitive latency is unacceptable.15
To break this sequential bottleneck, system architectures deploy speculative acceleration
frameworks, such as SpecEyes.15 The core innovation here involves utilizing a lightweight,
tool-free MLLM to act as a speculative planner.15 This smaller model rapidly predicts the likely
execution trajectory and the spatial location of the major layers. This prediction guides the
heavy, full-parameter MLLM, allowing it to bypass exhaustive pixel-by-pixel semantic correlation
and focus its heavy computational power solely on resolving the complex, ambiguous
boundaries, such as separating the Buffer layer from the Poly-Si.8 This speculative approach
significantly accelerates the generation of the labeled output image.
High-Precision Segmentation: From CNNs to Vision
Foundation Models
Semantic labeling represents only the initial phase of the requested workflow. To execute the
future steps of estimating layer thickness and detecting anomalies, the system must transition
from merely placing text labels to extracting precise, pixel-perfect mathematical boundaries for
every identified layer.20
The Degradation of Legacy Convolutional Architectures

Historically, the semiconductor industry relied heavily on Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNNs), such as Faster R-CNN, SegNet, and U-Net, to automate defect classification and
semantic segmentation.11 While these architectures demonstrate proficiency in extracting visual
features from large, homogeneous datasets, they suffer from debilitating limitations when
applied to the dynamic realities of modern multi-layer film stacks.11
CNNs are fundamentally limited by their demand for massive, exhaustively annotated training
datasets, often requiring thousands of labeled examples per specific defect class or layer
structure.11 When a manufacturing line transitions from an LTPS display array to an advanced
logic node featuring Hafnium Zirconium Oxide (HZO) ferroelectric capacitors, the visual
paradigm shifts completely.24 An HZO stack involves entirely different contrast dynamics,
featuring ultra-thin 6 nm HZO layers sandwiched between Titanium Nitride (TiN) and Platinum

(Pt) electrodes, often complicated by atomic-scale interfacial oxides like        or       .24 A
CNN trained on LTPS arrays will catastrophically fail on an HZO stack, requiring extensive and
costly retraining.11 Furthermore, legacy CNNs operate with a limited semantic understanding;
they classify patterns based on pixel gradients without any cognitive comprehension of the
underlying material physics, making them incapable of utilizing tabular supporting data.11
The Implementation of the Segment Anything Model (SAM-2)

To resolve these severe structural limitations, modern agentic workflows discard legacy CNNs in
favor of Vision Foundation Models (VFMs), most notably the Segment Anything Model (SAM-2)
and its derivatives.10 SAM-2 is engineered for open-vocabulary, zero-shot, and one-shot
segmentation, completely eliminating the need for domain-specific retraining.9
The integration between the MLLM and SAM-2 operates through a seamless prompt-mask
cycle.9 Once the MLLM has deduced the sequence and approximate location of the layers
within the red rectangle, it autonomously generates targeted prompts for SAM-2. These prompts
can take the form of textual descriptors (e.g., "Segment the bright metallic band corresponding
to SD2") or geometric point prompts (e.g., feeding the exact pixel coordinates representing the
center of the GE1 layer).9 SAM-2 receives these prompts and dynamically generates highly
accurate binary segmentation masks that precisely trace the topographical contours of the
layers across the entire width of the micrograph.26

Despite its immense power, applying a general-purpose model like SAM-2 to high-noise
electron micrographs requires specialized adaptation. Unmodified SAM models can struggle
with the inherently weak semantic cues of subtle semiconductor structures, often resulting in
scattered, false-positive binarization.10 To optimize boundary extraction, specialized frameworks
such as IC-DefectNCD deploy Self-Saliency-driven Adaptive Binarization.10 This technique
forces the model to mine intra-image cues and residual localizations, ensuring that the final
segmentation masks adhere strictly to the physical material interfaces, effectively filtering out
the ambient noise inherent in low-dose TEM or SEM imaging.10 For layers like the organic PLN
sequences where physical boundaries are truly non-existent, the system interpolates the
boundary mask based strictly on the proportional mathematical distribution derived from the
MLLM's analysis of the design table.9

Metrological Scale Calibration and Optical Character
Recognition
The extraction of precise mathematical segmentation masks remains fundamentally useless
unless those masks can be mapped to physical dimensions. To execute the future step of
estimating layer thickness, the automated system must possess an inherent understanding of
the micrograph's spatial scale. In human-driven workflows, the engineer simply reads the scale

bar permanently burned into the footer of the image (e.g., the "2  " scale bar clearly visible
in the LTPS array image) and manually calibrates the measurement software.5 For an
autonomous MLLM agent, this requires the deployment of a highly robust Optical Character
Recognition (OCR) and text verification pipeline.5
LLM-Assisted Scale Bar Extraction Frameworks

The automated extraction of scale bars from SEM and TEM images is a complex multi-modal
task due to varying fonts, background noise, and heterogeneous image formats.5 Recent
advancements have culminated in the development of multi-modal, LLM-assisted scale bar
detection frameworks that operate in discrete, highly optimized phases.5
The process initiates with an automated object detection module specifically trained on synthetic
datasets generated by Auto-DG algorithms.5 This module scans the entire micrograph and
draws a bounding box exclusively around the physical scale bar line and its adjacent
alphanumeric text, achieving exceptional localization metrics, including 100% precision and a
mean Average Precision (mAP) of 99.2% at an Intersection over Union (IoU) of 0.5.5
Once the scale region is isolated, the system deploys a hybrid OCR engine utilizing advanced
algorithms such as DenseNet and Convolutional Recurrent Neural Networks (CRNN) to extract
the text.5 This hybrid approach significantly outperforms standalone legacy OCR engines,
delivering an 89% precision rate on scientific images.5 However, OCR algorithms are notoriously
susceptible to misinterpreting single characters, particularly confusing "nm" (nanometers) with

"mm" (millimeters) or misreading the Greek letter " " (microns).5
To guarantee absolute metrological accuracy, the extracted text is passed to an LLM reasoning
agent. The LLM evaluates the OCR output against the visual context of the image and its
general knowledge of semiconductor physics.5 If the OCR outputs "10 mm" for a high-resolution
TEM image showing individual atomic lattice fringes, the LLM recognizes the physical
impossibility of the scale and autonomously corrects the error to "10 nm", verifying the result
before passing it to the measurement backend.5 Alternatively, if the original image is provided as
a proprietary digital micrograph (DM) file, Python libraries such as SimpliPyTEM can be directed
by the agent to bypass OCR entirely and extract the precise pixel-size calibration directly from
the embedded metadata.34

Algorithmic Thickness Metrology and Physics-Based
Modeling
With the micrograph fully calibrated and the distinct material layers accurately segmented by
SAM-2, the agentic workflow proceeds to the rigorous mathematical estimation of layer
thickness.21 It is crucial to note that at the microscopic level, semiconductor interfaces are rarely
perfectly planar.38 Effects such as interface roughness, diffusion, and intermixing ensure that a
single point-to-point distance measurement is statistically invalid.38
Gray-Level Intensity Profiles

To achieve robust, reproducible thickness estimation, the automated system relies on the
generation of vertical line scans, also known as gray-level intensity profiles.21
The MLLM instructs the Python image processing backend—typically utilizing libraries such as
OpenCV or SciPy—to cast a dense array of parallel vertical lines across the entire width of the
segmented layer within the red rectangle.35 As a virtual line traverses downwards through the
image, the software records the grayscale pixel intensity at every point. When the line crosses a
boundary from one material to another—for example, transitioning from the heavy Molybdenum

GE1 layer to the lighter            GI1 layer in the LTPS array—the electron scattering
cross-section abruptly changes, resulting in a distinct shift in the plotted intensity curve.40
The precise mathematical boundaries between the layers are not determined by simple
thresholding, but rather by calculating the inflection points of the smoothed intensity profile using
first and second-order derivatives.21 The distance between these calculated inflection points
constitutes the thickness of the layer at that specific vertical slice. By aggregating hundreds of
these parallel profiles, the system calculates a highly accurate mean thickness, alongside
critical statistical variance metrics including standard deviation, minimum/maximum thickness,
and the root-mean-square roughness of the interface.38
Compensating for Probe Broadening with JMONSEL

While intensity profiles are sufficient for macroscopic structures like the massive 2700 nm
organic PLN2/3 layers, estimating the thickness of ultra-thin films—such as the 6 nm Hafnium
Zirconium Oxide (HZO) layers or the sub-20 nm logic gate oxides—requires an understanding of
electron beam physics.12
When a high-energy electron beam penetrates a relatively thick sample, elastic scattering
causes the interaction volume of the probe to broaden as it travels deeper in the Z-direction.47
Consequently, the secondary electron intensity profile at an edge becomes dependent on both
the layer's true width and the specific scattering characteristics of the material, causing narrow
lines to appear blurred and artificially thick.46
To counteract this, the most advanced MLLM metrology pipelines integrate physics-based
simulation engines, such as the Java Monte Carlo Simulation of Secondary Electrons
(JMONSEL).46 Instead of relying solely on the raw image data, the system utilizes JMONSEL to
generate a theoretical library of predicted electron yield profiles based on the sample's known
geometry and composition.46 The metrology backend models the central peak of the measured
profile using a Gaussian function and describes the scattering tails using exponential decay
functions.47 The software iteratively adjusts these shape parameters until the theoretical model
perfectly aligns with the empirical measurement.46 This profound integration of computational
physics ensures that thickness estimation achieves true nanometer-scale accuracy, mitigating
the resolution limits imposed by beam broadening and signal-to-noise degradation.38

Autonomous Anomaly and Defect Detection
The ultimate objective of the requested workflow is anomaly detection. In the context of
semiconductor failure analysis, anomalies are not limited to catastrophic mechanical failures like
delamination; they encompass a vast spectrum of subtle morphological deviations, including
interfacial voiding, unexpected crystallographic phase transitions, and improper film
stoichiometries.28
Visual Identification of Morphological Defects

When the MLLM surveys the segmented image, it leverages its instruction-tuned knowledge
base to identify visual anomalies that deviate from the expected baseline.10 Within the context of
the LTPS array, the model might identify a continuity break in the Ti/Al/Ti SD1 interconnect,
suggesting a catastrophic open-circuit defect resulting from aggressive etching. Alternatively,
when analyzing a high-k/metal gate stack, the model can detect the presence of unwanted

interfacial oxides, such as a localized        formation resulting from oxygen scavenging at a

                  interface during thermal annealing.24
Furthermore, the system can identify Strong Metal-Support Interactions (SMSI), where partially
reduced oxide species migrate to the surface of a metal layer, fundamentally altering the
expected grayscale contrast and indicating severe thermal degradation.51 By utilizing
open-vocabulary foundation models like RF-DETR or SAM-2, the anomaly detection module is
not constrained to a predefined list of known flaws; it can flag any localized morphological
aberration or structural asymmetry as a potential defect for further review.10
Agentic Frameworks for Root-Cause Prognostics

Identifying the physical presence of a defect is merely the diagnostic phase; determining the
origin of the defect requires sophisticated root-cause analysis.1 The most profound application
of MLLMs in this domain is their orchestration within multi-agent LangGraph pipelines, such as
the SemiFA framework, which transforms simple image inspection into holistic factory
intelligence.1
The SemiFA architecture decomposes the failure analysis process into a sequence of
specialized AI agents:
   1.​ DefectDescriber: Utilizing DINOv2 vision embeddings and an MLLM like LLaVA-1.6, this
        agent classifies the visual morphology of the defect identified in the SEM cross-section,
        generating a structured narrative of the anomaly.1
   2.​ RootCauseAnalyzer: This agent represents the nexus of multimodal fusion. It takes the
        visual data and narrative from the DefectDescriber and correlates it with external,
        non-visual equipment telemetry, such as SECS/GEM data generated by the
        manufacturing tools.1 It also queries a Qdrant vector database to retrieve historically
        similar defects.1
  3.​ SeverityClassifier: Based on the location and magnitude of the defect (e.g., a massive
      void in the Anode versus a minor variation in the Buffer layer), this agent calculates a
      severity score and estimates the potential downstream yield impact.1
  4.​ RecipeAdvisor: The final agent utilizes the aggregated data to propose highly specific
      corrective actions, suggesting adjustments to process parameters—such as altering the
      deposition temperature or reactive gas flow rates in an Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD)
      chamber—to prevent the recurrence of the defect.1

For example, if the algorithmic thickness metrology reveals that the          PV layer is 200 nm
instead of its target 350 nm, and the DefectDescriber identifies high surface roughness, the
RootCauseAnalyzer can cross-reference this data with SECS/GEM logs to identify a pressure
anomaly in the plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition (PECVD) tool.1 This capability
elevates the MLLM from a simple image processing utility into an autonomous, proactive
yield-management system.52

Second and Third-Order Strategic Implications
The successful deployment of MLLM-driven automated labeling, metrology, and anomaly
detection introduces several profound second and third-order implications that will
fundamentally alter the operational landscape of semiconductor manufacturing.
The most immediate consequence is the complete displacement of the traditional failure
analysis bottleneck. Historically, human cognitive latency—the hours required to interpret a
micrograph—limited the throughput of the FA laboratory.1 By automating the extraction of
structural data and generating comprehensive FA reports in under a minute 1, the analytical
bottleneck is eliminated. This shifts the operational pressure entirely to data acquisition, strongly
incentivizing fabs to invest in high-throughput, fully automated SEM and TEM robotics capable
of feeding a continuous stream of cross-sectional images to the MLLM pipeline.15
Furthermore, these agentic frameworks catalyze the democratization of advanced materials
characterization. Extracting deep insights from high-resolution electron microscopy historically
required the expertise of a PhD-level crystallographer.8 Platforms like MicroscopyGPT and
ChemMiner codify this highly specialized knowledge, embedding it directly into the accessible
interface of a Large Language Model.8 This empowers standard process engineers to extract
complex phase information, atomic coordinate reconstructions, and root-cause analyses simply
by querying the system with an image and a natural language prompt, massively expanding the
analytical bandwidth of the organization.8
Finally, the transition to physics-based algorithmic metrology, driven by models like JMONSEL,
enforces a level of statistical rigor and reproducibility that effectively eliminates the subjectivity
inherent in human-driven measurements.38 As device geometries continue to scale into the
angstrom era, this guaranteed metrological precision, coupled with the open-vocabulary
adaptability of MLLMs, ensures that manufacturers can maintain stringent process controls,
accelerate the yield curve for novel architectures, and secure a dominant position in the
increasingly complex landscape of microelectronic fabrication.
